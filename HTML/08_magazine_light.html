Feature March 2025

The Art of Slow Living

In our always-on culture, intentional slowness is becoming the ultimate luxury. Here's how to embrace it.

Emma Richardson

Senior Lifestyle Editor

There's a quiet revolution happening in homes around the world. It doesn't involve technology or productivity hacks. Instead, it's about something far more radical: the deliberate choice to slow down.

The slow living movement, which has its roots in the slow food philosophy that began in Italy in the 1980s, has evolved into a comprehensive approach to life that touches everything from how we eat to how we work, rest, and connect with others.

For decades, we've been sold the idea that faster is better. Quick meals. Rapid responses. Instant gratification. But a growing number of people are pushing back against this culture of speed, choosing instead to prioritize depth over breadth, quality over quantity.

lifestyle 6

What Is Slow Living?

Slow living is a lifestyle philosophy that emphasizes doing things at the right speed - savoring moments, being present, and prioritizing quality over quantity in all aspects of life.

"It's not about doing everything slowly," explains Dr. Catherine Price, author of "How to Break Up with Your Phone." "It's about doing things at the right speed, with intention and presence."

"The great benefit of slowing down is reclaiming the time and tranquility to make meaningful connections."
- Carl Honore, Author of "In Praise of Slowness"

The appeal of slow living has only grown in recent years, accelerated paradoxically by the pandemic. When the world stopped, many of us were forced to confront the unsustainable pace of our lives. We baked bread, planted gardens, and rediscovered the pleasure of unhurried meals with family.

Now, as life has returned to "normal," many are reluctant to give up those moments of slowness. A 2024 survey found that 67% of respondents said they actively wanted to slow down their pace of life - up from 48% just five years earlier.

But what does slow living actually look like in practice? It's not about moving to the countryside or quitting your job (though for some, it might be). It's about making conscious choices in the life you already have.

It might mean taking the time to cook a meal from scratch instead of ordering delivery. It could be putting your phone in another room while you read a book. It's choosing to walk instead of drive when you can, or saying no to plans so you can have an unscheduled weekend.

Small changes can make a significant difference. Consider starting your day without immediately reaching for your phone. Or establishing a weekly ritual - a Sunday morning farmers market visit, an evening walk, a phone-free dinner with loved ones.

Getting Started with Slow Living

Begin with just one area of your life. Maybe it's meals - committing to eating without screens, or cooking one meal a week from scratch. Once that feels natural, expand to other areas. The goal isn't perfection; it's intention.

The benefits extend beyond personal well-being. Research shows that people who practice mindful, unhurried living report higher levels of life satisfaction, better relationships, and improved mental health. They're also more likely to engage with their communities and feel connected to others.

In a world that constantly demands more, faster, now - choosing to slow down is perhaps the most radical act of self-care. It's a declaration that your time and attention are precious, and that you get to decide how to spend them.

As Carl Honore wisely notes, "The slow philosophy is not about doing everything at a snail's pace. It's about seeking to do everything at the right speed. Savoring the hours and minutes rather than just counting them."

Finding Balance in the Digital Age

Our devices are designed to capture our attention. Here's how to reclaim it.

James Park

Technology Writer

When was the last time you went an entire day without checking your phone? For most of us, the honest answer is: we can't remember. Our smartphones have become extensions of ourselves, always within reach, always demanding attention.

The average person now checks their phone 96 times a day - that's once every ten minutes during waking hours. We scroll through social media for nearly two and a half hours daily. We feel phantom vibrations in our pockets. We experience genuine anxiety when our battery drops below 20%.

This isn't an accident. Our devices and the apps on them are engineered by some of the smartest minds in the world to capture and hold our attention. Every notification, every infinite scroll, every autoplay video is designed to keep us engaged.

lifestyle 10
"Technology should work for us, not the other way around. The goal isn't to quit tech - it's to use it intentionally."
- Cal Newport, Digital Minimalism

But there's a growing movement of people who are pushing back. They're not anti-technology; they're pro-intention. They use digital tools when they add value to their lives, and set boundaries when they don't.

The approach varies from person to person. Some establish phone-free zones in their homes - the bedroom, the dinner table. Others schedule specific times to check email and social media, rather than responding to every notification in real-time.

Mindset March 2025

The Joy of Imperfection

Why embracing our flaws might be the path to greater happiness and creativity.

Sarah Chen

Contributing Writer

In Japan, there's an ancient art called kintsugi - the practice of repairing broken pottery with gold. Rather than hiding the cracks, this technique highlights them, celebrating the history and imperfection of each piece.

It's a beautiful philosophy that stands in stark contrast to our modern obsession with perfection. We curate flawless Instagram feeds, pursue impossible beauty standards, and beat ourselves up over every mistake.

But what if we have it backwards? What if our imperfections aren't obstacles to overcome, but essential parts of who we are?

lifestyle 14
[KINTSUGI POTTERY IMAGE]

Kintsugi transforms broken pottery into art - Photo by Yuki Tanaka

Research in psychology supports this shift in perspective. Studies show that people who embrace imperfection - who practice self-compassion rather than self-criticism - actually perform better, are more resilient, and report higher levels of well-being.

The Wabi-Sabi Philosophy

Wabi-sabi is a Japanese aesthetic centered on accepting the beauty of imperfection, impermanence, and incompleteness. It finds value in the simple, the worn, the authentic.

The pursuit of perfection, paradoxically, often leads to mediocrity. Fear of making mistakes keeps us from taking risks, trying new things, and learning. It's in our failures and flaws that we often find our greatest growth.

"Done is better than perfect" has become a mantra for a reason. The painting you finish imperfectly is infinitely more valuable than the masterpiece you never start.

Interview

Maya Collins on Modern Wellness

The bestselling author and wellness advocate shares her thoughts on self-care, balance, and what we get wrong about health.

Your new book challenges a lot of conventional wellness wisdom. What inspired that?

I got tired of the perfectionism that's crept into wellness culture. The green smoothies, the 5 AM workouts, the obsession with optimization. It all started feeling like another way to fail. Real wellness should make your life bigger, not smaller.

What's the biggest misconception about self-care?

That it has to look a certain way. Self-care for one person might be a bubble bath; for another, it might be setting a boundary with a difficult family member. It's not about the aesthetic - it's about actually taking care of yourself.

How do you practice balance in your own life?

Imperfectly! I think that's the point. I try to move my body most days, eat in a way that makes me feel good, and protect my sleep. But I also eat pizza and stay up too late watching shows. Balance isn't about perfect equilibrium.

lifestyle 18

You've talked about the "wellness industrial complex." What do you mean?

There's a whole industry profiting from making us feel inadequate. Selling us solutions to problems we didn't know we had. I'm not against supplements or workout programs, but we need to be aware of the marketing around them.

What advice would you give someone just starting their wellness journey?

Start with what you already enjoy. Don't force yourself into a routine you hate because someone on Instagram says it's good for you. Also, question the word "should." Every time you think "I should do X," ask yourself why. Whose voice is that?

What's one thing you wish everyone knew about mental health?

That it's not separate from physical health, and that it fluctuates. Having a bad mental health day doesn't mean you've failed or gone backwards. It means you're human. The goal isn't to never feel bad - it's to know you can handle it when you do.

"Real wellness should make your life bigger, not smaller."
- Maya Collins

Spring Recipe

Lemon Herb Roasted Chicken

A classic weekend dish that fills your home with irresistible aromas. Served with spring vegetables.

15m
Prep
1h
Cook
4
Serves

Ingredients

  • 1 whole chicken (3-4 lbs)
  • 2 lemons
  • 4 cloves garlic
  • Fresh thyme & rosemary
  • 4 tbsp olive oil
  • Salt & pepper
  • 1 lb baby potatoes
  • 1 bunch asparagus
  • 1 cup cherry tomatoes

Method

1

Preheat oven to 425F. Pat chicken dry with paper towels. Season generously inside and out with salt and pepper.

2

Halve one lemon and stuff inside the cavity along with garlic and half the herbs. Tie legs together.

3

Rub chicken with olive oil. Place in roasting pan surrounded by potatoes. Roast for 45 minutes.

4

Add asparagus and tomatoes. Continue roasting 15-20 minutes until chicken reaches 165F.

Quick & Easy

Spring Pea Risotto

Creamy, comforting, and brightened with fresh peas and mint. The perfect weeknight dinner.

10m
Prep
30m
Cook
4
Serves

Ingredients

  • 1.5 cups arborio rice
  • 5 cups vegetable stock
  • 1 cup fresh peas
  • 1 shallot, minced
  • 1/2 cup white wine
  • 1/2 cup parmesan
  • 3 tbsp butter
  • Fresh mint leaves
  • Lemon zest

Method

1

Keep stock warm in a separate pot. Saute shallot in butter until soft. Add rice, toast 1-2 minutes.

2

Add wine, stir until absorbed. Add stock one ladle at a time, stirring often.

3

After about 18 minutes, add peas. Continue cooking until rice is creamy but still has bite.

4

Remove from heat. Stir in parmesan, remaining butter, lemon zest. Top with fresh mint.

Morning Routines That Actually Work

01

Delay Your Phone

Wait at least 30 minutes before checking your phone. Give yourself time to set intentions before the world rushes in.

02

Hydrate First

Start with a large glass of water. Your body has been fasting for hours and needs hydration before caffeine.

03

Move Your Body

Even 10 minutes of movement - stretching, walking, yoga - can transform your energy for the entire day.

04

Morning Pages

Write three pages of stream-of-consciousness journaling. It clears mental clutter and sparks creativity.

05

Eat a Real Breakfast

Prioritize protein and fiber. A nourishing breakfast stabilizes energy and mood throughout the morning.

06

Set Three Intentions

Before you start work, identify three things you want to accomplish. Clarity leads to productivity.
