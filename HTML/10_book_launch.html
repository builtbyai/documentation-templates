Chapter One

The Inheritance

~ * ~

The letter arrived on a Tuesday, as all life-changing letters do. Clara had always believed that Tuesdays were the universe's way of keeping us humble - not quite the fresh start of Monday, not yet the hump of Wednesday. Just ordinary. Unremarkable. The perfect camouflage for fate.

She found it tucked between an electricity bill and a catalog for garden supplies she'd never ordered, the envelope thick and cream-colored, bearing a law firm's embossed return address. The handwriting, however, was unmistakably personal. Elegant loops and careful curves that spoke of fountain pens and patience - qualities her grandmother had possessed in abundance.

Clara's fingers trembled as she broke the wax seal. A wax seal, in this age of email and instant messages. How very like Gran to insist on ceremony even from beyond the grave.

The letter was brief, as Gran's communications always were. She had believed in economy of words, in letting silence speak where language faltered. But what it contained changed everything.

"Willowmere is yours now," the attorney's accompanying note explained. "As per your grandmother's wishes, the estate passes to you alone, along with the garden and all its contents. Mrs. Ashworth left specific instructions that only you would understand."

Only you would understand. The words echoed in Clara's mind as she stood in her cramped apartment kitchen, morning light filtering through windows that hadn't been properly cleaned in months. She hadn't set foot in Willowmere since she was seventeen. Hadn't spoken to Gran in over a decade. Hadn't allowed herself to think about the garden - that impossible, magical garden - since the night everything went wrong.

Yet somehow, Gran had known. Had always known that Clara would return when the time was right.

The Midnight Garden Chapter One

The drive north took seven hours, each mile carrying Clara further from the life she'd carefully constructed and closer to the one she'd abandoned. Her sensible sedan ate up the highway, radio playing songs she didn't hear, mind wandering through memories she'd spent years suppressing.

She remembered summer nights in Gran's garden, fireflies dancing between the hedgerows, the sweet perfume of jasmine and moonflower filling the air. She remembered the fountain at the garden's heart, its waters silver in the moonlight, and the stories Gran would tell about the generations of Ashworth women who had tended this place.

"The garden keeps secrets," Gran had once said, her weathered hands pruning a rose bush with practiced ease. "Our secrets, our sorrows, our dreams. We plant them here, and the garden transforms them into something beautiful."

At seventeen, Clara had thought she understood. At thirty-four, she suspected she hadn't understood anything at all.

The iron gates of Willowmere appeared as dusk settled over the countryside, their elaborate scrollwork silhouetted against a salmon-pink sky. They stood open, as if expecting her, though the attorney had assured Clara the property had been empty since Gran's passing three months ago.

The drive curved through an avenue of ancient oaks, their branches intertwining overhead to form a living tunnel. In her memory, this approach had always felt like entering another world - a threshold between the ordinary and the extraordinary. Now, with the evening light turning everything to gold and shadow, that feeling returned with startling intensity.

And then she saw the house.

Willowmere stood exactly as she remembered, a Victorian beauty in soft gray stone with white trim and windows that caught the dying light like watchful eyes. But it was what lay beyond the house that made Clara's breath catch in her throat.

The garden was waiting.

About the Author

Eleanor Blackwood

Eleanor Blackwood grew up in a rambling Victorian house in Cornwall, where her grandmother tended a garden that seemed to hold as many secrets as flowers. Those childhood summers spent among the moonflowers and midnight roses would later inspire her internationally acclaimed fiction.

After studying literature at Oxford and spending a decade as a journalist in London, Eleanor retreated to the English countryside to write her first novel. The result, "The House at Thornwood," became an instant bestseller and established her as a master of atmospheric literary fiction.

Her work has been translated into thirty-seven languages and has won numerous awards, including the Women's Prize for Fiction and the Costa Novel Award. Critics have praised her "luminous prose" and her ability to find magic in the everyday.

Eleanor lives in Devon with her husband, two children, and a garden that grows more mysterious with each passing year. The Midnight Garden is her sixth novel.

When Clara Ashworth inherits her estranged grandmother's estate, she returns to Willowmere expecting dusty rooms and painful memories. Instead, she finds a garden that defies explanation - flowers that bloom only by moonlight, paths that seem to shift and change, and secrets planted generations ago that are finally ready to bloom.

As Clara uncovers the truth about her family and the extraordinary women who came before her, she must confront the tragedy that drove her away and the magic she tried so hard to forget. But some gardens grow wild when left untended, and some secrets, once unearthed, can never be buried again.
